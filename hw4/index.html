Part 1: Masses and springs

We built the cloth as a grid of point masses stored in row-major order and connected them with structural, shearing, and bending springs. For each grid location, we created a PointMass, marked it as pinned if its coordinate appeared in the scene's pinned list, and then added springs only after all masses had been created so the spring endpoints could safely store pointers into the mass array. Structural springs connect each point mass to the mass above and to the left, shearing springs connect diagonal upper-left and upper-right neighbors, and bending springs connect masses that are two away horizontally or vertically.

The screenshots below show the wireframe for scene/pinned2.json under different constraint settings. Without shearing constraints, the cloth only shows horizontal and vertical connections. With only shearing constraints enabled, the wireframe becomes a diagonal lattice. With all constraints enabled, the cloth contains the full combination of structural, shearing, and bending springs, producing the densest and most stable support structure.

Part 2: Simulation via numerical integration

In this part, we implemented force accumulation, Verlet integration, and the Provot-style spring length constraint. That gave the cloth a stable time-step update: external forces and spring forces are accumulated first, point masses are advanced with Verlet, and then overly stretched springs are corrected so the cloth does not explode numerically.

Default parameters

These are the baseline settings we used for comparison unless otherwise noted:

Effect of spring constant ks

With a very low ks, the cloth behaves like a loose fabric or a sheet of cloth with weak structural support. It stretches a lot under gravity, sags more dramatically, and takes longer to settle into a resting shape. With a high ks, the cloth becomes much stiffer and resists deformation, so it keeps its overall shape better but can also look more tense and can oscillate more aggressively if the damping is not high enough.

Effect of density

Changing density changes the mass of each point mass, which changes how strongly gravity accelerates the cloth relative to the spring forces. A lower density cloth feels lighter and settles with less sag, while a higher density cloth droops more, moves with more inertia, and tends to swing through larger motions before reaching rest.

These screenshots compare the start and end of the simulation for two density settings. The low-density cloth falls with less inertia and settles with a lighter drape. The high-density cloth sags more aggressively and keeps more momentum before reaching its resting shape.

Effect of damping

Damping controls how quickly motion loses energy from one time step to the next. With low damping, the cloth keeps bouncing and swaying for a long time before it settles. With high damping, the motion dies out faster and the cloth reaches rest much sooner, but if it is too high the motion can look overly suppressed and less natural.

These screenshots compare two damping settings. The cloth with damping 0.11 descended much more quickly because it lost kinetic energy faster each step, so the motion was damped out sooner. The cloth with damping 0.77 retained more of its motion for longer, so it fell and settled more slowly with more visible lingering movement.

Part 3: Handling collisions with other objects

Sphere collisions: To handle collisions with the sphere we first check if a point mass is inside the sphere by comparing its distance from the origin to the radius. If the point mass is inside the sphere, we compute the tangent point by starting from the origin, going through the point mass's position, then out to the sphere surface. We then make a correction vector from the point mass's last position to this tangent point and apply it scaled by (1 - f) to get the new position.

For plane collisions, we take the dot product of each point mass's position and last position with the plane normal to get their signed distances from the plane. If the signs are the same, the point mass is still on the same side of the plane, otherwise the point mass crossed the plane during this timestep. We find the tangent point by projecting the current position onto the plane along the normal, then bump it slightly back using the surface offset given. We compute the correction from last position to this adjusted tangent point and scale by (1 - friction).

In both cases, the collision loop in simulate iterates over every point mass and every collision object each timestep. The general approach follows the instructions given in the assignment.

With ks = 500, the cloth drapes very loosely around the sphere, and hangs pretty far down with a lot of folds (since the springs are too weak). At the default ks = 5000, the cloth sits more better on the sphere with fewer folds and and feels more natural with the droopiness. With ks = 50000, the cloth is much stiffer and sits really stiffly on the sphere. It is more like a stiff paper than matching the curvature of the sphere.

Part 4: Handling self-collisions

For self-collisions, we use spatial hashing to avoid the expensive \(O(n^2)\) pairwise check. Each timestep, we partition 3D space into boxes and hash every point mass into one based on its position. To hash, we truncate coordinates to the same box point for all other points in that box (the corner here) using modulo and combine them with random large numbers to get something unique.

Then for each point mass, if there are other point masses in the same box, and is less than 2 * thickness, we compute a correction vector pointing away from it. We skip the point mass itself so it doesn't collide with itself. After looking at all candidates, we avg the correction vectors and scale down by simulation_steps (to smooth), then apply it to the pm's current position.

All images are about a second after initial collision. The higher density cloth (30 g/cm²) comes to a restful state much more quickly due to being denser, gravity pulls it down harder so it settles faster. The lower ks cloth (500 N/m) has much more folds than the baseline because the weaker springs makes the cloth fold over itself on its own weight. The baseline (15 g/cm², 5000 N/m) sits between the two, with a moderate number of folds and a moderate settling time.
